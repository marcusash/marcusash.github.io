The setup

Why this conversation, why now

Most conversations about AI at work are either too abstract or too early. People talk about what AI could do. What is rarer is two practitioners comparing what it actually looks like to ship things with AI on the team, day after day, at real companies with real stakes.

Marcus and Greg are both past the point of piloting. The experiments are over. The questions that remain are harder: What do you actually hand to an agent? What falls apart when no one is watching? How do you maintain quality across a system you did not write by hand? What have you changed your mind about?

The question underneath everything

What does it look like to build products, tools, and systems with AI as a real team member, not just a feature?

This is not a general conversation about AI trends. It is a practitioner debrief. Marcus will show Forge. Greg will show ExpertVoice. Both of you will leave with something concrete.

Context

What Forge is

Forge is a 13-agent system Marcus runs inside his GitHub workspace. Each agent has a name, a color, an inbox, a sprint queue, a skills file, and a performance record. Agents communicate through structured JSON messages. They build real things: code, design systems, HTML, architecture decisions, test suites.

The agents are organized like a real company. Chief Architect. Design Director. Quality Lead. Research Lead. Chief of Operations. Security and Platform. Each owns a domain. Each reports to Marcus.

Forge runs autonomously overnight. Marcus reviews in the morning, approves what shipped, sends feedback on what did not. The communication is formal. Everything goes through the inbox protocol. There is no verbal escalation path.

Context

The hard problems are not technical

The tooling works. What is hard is organizational. How do you onboard a new agent without it drifting from the system's established conventions? How do you catch quality problems before they compound across 13 agents? How do you run performance reviews when the team cannot argue back?

"I am not using AI to write emails faster. I am managing a team that happens to be AI."

What works

Formal protocols. Structured inboxes. Explicit skill files. Sprint queues with clear acceptance criteria. Agents that stay in their lane. Nightly autonomous runs with morning review.

What is still being figured out

Context window limits on long sessions. Quality drift when agents go autonomous for too long. Knowing when to trust the output versus when to read the code. The eval problem: what does good actually look like?
