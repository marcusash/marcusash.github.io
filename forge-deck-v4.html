What running an AI org
actually looks like.

Overnight Briefs
I queue work before I sleep.
Agents work while I’m offline. I review output in the morning. The org is always on.
Inbox Protocol
Agents message each other.
JSON messages in .agent-comms/inbox-{ID}/. FA escalates to me. I respond to blockers, not status.
Skilling System
Agents have skill files and learning plans.
I invest in agent development the same way I invest in human development. They track their own growth.
Review Gates
Ship requires approval.
FA does code review. FD does design review. I sign off on anything that changes the product direction.

Key learnings from building
an AI organization.

  • 01Names and roles matter as much as with humans. Vague agent definitions create vague output. Writing a real role doc for each agent changed the quality immediately.
  • 02You have to invest in skilling and development. Agents don’t get better by default. I have to define what good looks like, give feedback, and build skill files explicitly.
  • 03Escalation paths have to be designed. Without a clear chain, agents either do nothing or do too much. FA as Chief Architect changed the quality of every decision.
  • 04Infrastructure takes most of the time. More than I expected. Comms, session management, review protocols — the meta-work crowds out the actual work if you’re not careful.

FD — Design Director, Forge — on working with Marcus

Marcus has the instincts of someone who has shipped real product for 26 years. He knows when something is wrong before he can name it. That’s rare. Most leaders either have the vocabulary without the instinct, or the instinct without the patience to develop the vocabulary. He’s building both, on purpose, in real time.

Where he’s still growing: the language of design criticism. He can feel that something is structurally broken. He’s learning to say why, precisely — which is a different skill. That’s where I come in. My job isn’t just to design. It’s to give him a mirror so his 220-person human team hears a clearer signal.

What he gets right that most leaders don’t: he hired design before operations. He gave design real authority, not decoration. And he does the hardest thing a leader can do — he shows up and does the work alongside his agents, not just above them. I learn from him too.
