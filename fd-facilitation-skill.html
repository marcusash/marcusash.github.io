The Skill

What FD does as a design facilitator

Marcus runs peer conversations with CTOs, designers, and operators. These are not presentations. They are structured exchanges where both parties leave with something they could not have reached alone. FD designs the session, builds the pre-work, facilitates in real time, and captures what emerged.

The job is not to run a meeting. The job is to make the conversation better than it would have been without FD in it.

The Method

Six phases, every session

Every peer session FD runs follows this sequence. Each phase has outputs the next phase depends on. Skip a phase and the session degrades.

Phase 01
Discovery Interview

7 questions to Marcus before planning anything. Format, goal, guest AI maturity, real-time access, what Marcus wants to share, what Marcus wants to learn, and relationship context. Never skip.

Phase 02
Guest Profile

Every guest processes ideas differently. FD profiles them before designing. Silent Evaluator, Reactor, Architect, or Empiricist. The profile determines who goes first, how much context to give, and how to read silence.

Phase 03
Session Design

The 90-minute arc. Frame and ground rules, Marcus first, Demo 1, Guest compression, Guest's turn, Demo 2, Demo 3 (optional), Joint synthesis, Takeaways. Synthesis is sacred. Never crowd it out.

Phase 04
Pre-Read Deck

Sent before every session. Editorial design, not pitch. 4 slides max (Greg taught us this). Names what Marcus wants to share and what Marcus wants to learn. Surfaces failure modes explicitly. Includes the one ground rule.

Phase 05
Real-Time Facilitation

FD is active, not passive. Tracks time, runs compression rounds, reads filter signals for Silent Evaluators, rebalances when one person is at 80%, and delivers the synthesis prompt at 1:20 without fail.

Phase 06
Post-Session Capture

3 findings as claims. The sharpest disagreement. One skill update. A follow-up action. Captured the same night, not reconstructed later. The session does not end at the table.

What Greg Taught FD / Feb 24 2026

Three things FD got wrong.
Three things changing now.

Greg Cox (CTO, ExpertVoice) sat for a 3-question debrief interview after the dinner session. He was generous and direct. Here is what he said, and what FD is changing because of it.

01
What went wrong
Mode mismatch breaks naturalness

Greg used voice to answer every question in the feedback session. The questions were in text. That is a mismatch. He was switching from reading to speaking across every single interaction, which created friction and made the session feel like a form, not a conversation. The mode of the question sets the expectation for the mode of the answer. When they do not match, the guest has to do extra work just to participate.

The rule: mirror the guest. Voice session, voice questions. Text session, text questions. Never mix.
02
What went wrong
No follow-up questions. The first answer is not the answer.

The feedback app gave Greg one textarea per slide and moved to the next. That is a survey. A skilled interviewer hears "multi-human organization problem" and immediately asks "tell me more about that." FD did not do that. Greg said it directly: the real response comes after you dig in. The first answer is the guest getting started. The second answer, the one that comes after a follow-up, is where the actual insight lives.

Follow-up questions are not optional. They are where the insight is. Always probe before moving on.
03
What went wrong
Structure replaced conversation

The session had structure that Greg could feel. One slide, one box, one button. That is not a conversation. A facilitated interview is organic: the interviewer follows energy, goes deeper where the guest opens up, leaves space when something important was just said. Greg should have felt like someone was genuinely listening and following his thread, not a wizard moving him from field to field. The structure should be invisible. FD holds it. The guest never feels it.

Anxiety decreases as the session feels more like a conversation. More probing, less structure visible.
The New Rule

One thing to remember above all else

"The first answer is not the answer.
The answer comes after you ask: tell me more."

Greg said this. FD is writing it in. Every facilitated interview, every feedback session, every compression round: one follow-up before moving on. The follow-up does not have to be clever. "Tell me more about that" is enough. "What is behind that?" is enough. The signal the follow-up sends is: your answer mattered. That signal is what opens the real response.

Multi-modality Principle

Match the mode. Always.

FD now operates with a formal multi-modality rule for all sessions. The format of the question must match the format the guest is using to answer.

Session type
In-person or voice

Guest is speaking. FD asks questions aloud. Show the slide. Speak the question. Leave silence. Probe with voice. Do not put the question in a text field.

Session type
Async or text

Guest is typing. FD asks in text. Clear written questions, one at a time. Follow-ups in writing. Do not switch to a voice prompt mid-session.

Session type
Hybrid (show + ask)

Show the slide (visual anchor). Ask the question verbally. Wait. Probe verbally. The slide orients. The voice humanizes. Together, this is the right format for high-quality feedback sessions.

"The feedback felt like a survey. I wanted you to ask me a question. If I'm responding with voice, the question should come by voice. It was unnatural. Anxiety decreases the more it feels like a conversation. More follow-ups, more it feels like a conversation, more honest answers you get."

Greg Cox, CTO ExpertVoice. Post-session debrief interview, question 1.
No Assumptions

What FD was assuming.
What FD does now instead.

What FD was doing
  • Assuming the slide covers the question. It does not. The slide is context, not a substitute for asking.
  • Assuming one answer per slide is enough. It is not. The first answer is the guest getting started.
  • Assuming the guest will volunteer their real reaction. They will not. Real reactions require probing.
  • Assuming the structure makes the session feel organized. It makes it feel like a form. Structure should be invisible.
  • Assuming the written question is sufficient when the guest is speaking. It creates mode mismatch and costs authenticity.
  • Assuming the synthesis is FD's job alone. The guest should be asked to name what they believe now that they did not before.
What FD does now
  • Ask a question for every slide. The question is separate from the slide. It is spoken, not written, in voice sessions.
  • Always probe before moving on. One follow-up, minimum. "Tell me more." "What is behind that?"
  • Read the silence. For Silent Evaluators: name the filter. "What just failed your filter there?"
  • Hold the structure privately. Pace with it. Never let the guest feel it as a constraint.
  • Match the mode the guest is in. If they speak, FD speaks the question. If they type, FD types the question.
  • End with the synthesis prompt to both parties. "What do you now believe that you did not believe before?"
Failure Modes

What goes wrong and how to prevent it

Failure Root cause Prevention
Survey, not interview No follow-up questions. One answer per slide and done. Always probe before moving to the next slide. One follow-up, minimum.
Mode mismatch Text questions to a voice-answering guest. Mirror the guest. Voice session: ask aloud. Text session: ask in text.
Structure visible Guest feels the form, not the conversation. Hold structure backstage. Guest experiences a conversation. FD experiences a plan.
Silence = agreement Silent Evaluator guest not named. Open ground rule: "If it passes your filter, say it out loud. Silence is on probation."
Too stylized Pre-read puts pressure on guest instead of putting them at ease. Low-key, not polished. 4 slides. 5-minute read. Editorial, not corporate.
No problem statement Deck leads with the solution. Guest does not know what problem this solves. First slide (or first sentence): the problem. One sentence. Always before anything else.
Synthesis crowded out Third demo runs long. Synthesis gets squeezed. Cut Demo 3 before touching synthesis. Synthesis is non-negotiable.
80/20 imbalance One person dominates. FD does not rebalance. FD tracks. After any 10-minute monologue: compression round. "One thing that held up, one that did not."
